# PROMPT_60: Organization Profiles — Research Labs & Universities

## Objective

Create the first batch of organization profiles covering the research
institutions and universities that were the birthplaces of foundational
programming languages and paradigms. Organizations do NOT require an entry
in a separate JSON file — the build pipeline creates the `organizations`
table row from the profile's `title` field if the org is not already present.

## Mandatory First Steps

1. Read `AGENT.md` and `CLAUDE.md`.
2. Load `.context/data-pipeline.md` (especially the org_profiles section).
3. Load `.context/architecture.md`.
4. Create `tmp/PROMPT_60_checklist.md` with a checkbox per profile.
5. Check off each item immediately after completing it.
6. Confirm `data/docs/org_profiles/` exists and is empty (or note what's there).

## How Organization Profiles Work

Files go in `data/docs/org_profiles/`. The filename is the organization
name with spaces replaced by underscores (e.g., `Bell_Labs.json`).

The build pipeline (`build_sqlite.py`) extracts the org name from `title`
(splitting on the first colon) and inserts a row into `organizations` if
one does not already exist. The `founded` field populates `organizations.founded`.

## Organizations to Profile

### 1. Bell Labs (`Bell_Labs.json`)

The single most productive research institution in computing history.

- **Founded:** 1925 (Bell Telephone Laboratories)
- **Key contributions:**
  - Transistor (1947, Shockley/Bardeen/Brattain) — without which no computers
  - Unix operating system (1969, Ritchie and Thompson)
  - C programming language (1972, Ritchie)
  - B language (Thompson), BCPL influence
  - Plan 9 (successor to Unix, 1987)
  - C++ was designed here by Stroustrup (while at Bell Labs)
  - AWK (Aho, Weinberger, Kernighan)
  - S language (statistical computing, ancestor of R)
  - AMPL (mathematical programming language)
  - Shannon's Information Theory (1948) — mathematical foundation of all digital communication
- **Key people:** Dennis Ritchie, Ken Thompson, Brian Kernighan, Rob Pike,
  Alfred Aho, Doug McIlroy, Joe Ossanna (troff), Bjarne Stroustrup
- **Legacy:** The "Bell Labs model" — deep research with long time horizons
  and no pressure to ship products — produced more foundational technology
  per researcher than any institution before or since

### 2. Xerox PARC (`Xerox_PARC.json`)

- **Founded:** 1970 (Palo Alto Research Center)
- **Key contributions:**
  - Smalltalk (1972, Alan Kay, Adele Goldberg, Dan Ingalls)
  - Graphical user interface (overlapping windows, icons, menus, pointer)
  - Ethernet networking (1973, Bob Metcalfe)
  - Laser printing
  - WYSIWYG text editing (Bravo editor)
  - Alto workstation — the first personal computer with a GUI
  - PostScript language precursor (Interpress)
  - Object-oriented programming as a paradigm (from Simula, realized in Smalltalk)
- **Key people:** Alan Kay, Adele Goldberg, Dan Ingalls, Butler Lampson,
  Charles Thacker, Bob Taylor
- **Legacy:** Steve Jobs famously visited in 1979 and took the GUI back to
  Apple. The tragedy of PARC is that Xerox failed to capitalize on its own
  inventions, but those inventions became the foundation of the personal
  computing era.

### 3. MIT (Massachusetts Institute of Technology) — AI Lab & Project MAC
   (`MIT.json`)

- **Founded:** 1861 (MIT); Project MAC 1963; AI Lab 1959 (as part of RLE)
- **Key contributions:**
  - LISP (1958, John McCarthy)
  - Scheme (1975, Sussman and Steele)
  - MULTICS (1969) — precursor concept to Unix
  - SICP (Structure and Interpretation of Computer Programs, 1985)
  - Emacs (Richard Stallman, 1976)
  - Scratch (programming language for children, 2003)
  - Dylan (Apple+CMU+MIT collaboration, dynamic OOP)
  - Influential research: type theory, program verification, distributed
    systems (Lamport was at MIT before DEC SRC)
- **Key people:** John McCarthy, Gerald Jay Sussman, Guy Steele,
  Richard Stallman, Hal Abelson

### 4. Carnegie Mellon University (`Carnegie_Mellon_University.json`)

- **Founded:** 1900 (as Carnegie Technical Schools); CS dept from 1960s
- **Key contributions:**
  - ML language family influence (Robin Milner visited/collaborated)
  - Standard ML of New Jersey (SML/NJ, David MacQueen, Robert Harper)
  - Common Lisp development (Scott Fahlman, CMU Common Lisp)
  - Ada compiler work
  - Software Engineering Institute (SEI) — CMMI, process models
  - Research: type theory (Bob Harper), proof assistants, formal verification
  - Mach OS (microkernel, influenced macOS XNU kernel)
- **Key people:** Allen Newell, Herbert Simon (Nobel — AI research),
  Robert Harper, David MacQueen, Scott Fahlman

### 5. Dartmouth College (`Dartmouth_College.json`)

- **Founded:** 1769; CS connection from 1950s
- **Key contributions:**
  - BASIC (1964, Kemeny and Kurtz) — designed for beginner programmers
    on the GE-225 timesharing system
  - Time-sharing computing as a concept for general users (not just experts)
  - DTSS (Dartmouth Time Sharing System) — one of the first usable
    interactive computing environments
- **Key people:** John G. Kemeny, Thomas E. Kurtz
- **Legacy:** BASIC's democratizing effect on computing is enormous.
  Every 8-bit home computer of the 1980s shipped with BASIC in ROM,
  introducing a generation to programming.

### 6. Stanford AI Lab (`Stanford_AI_Lab.json`)

Also known as SAIL (Stanford Artificial Intelligence Laboratory).

- **Founded:** 1963 (John McCarthy, after leaving MIT)
- **Key contributions:**
  - LISP implementations and dialects (MacLisp, Interlisp)
  - SAIL programming language (an ALGOL dialect with pattern matching)
  - Early work on natural language processing, robotics, vision
  - Pascal (Niklaus Wirth visited Stanford; his Pascal work overlapped
    with his Stanford years)
  - Fundamental AI research that shaped Prolog, expert systems, logic
    programming
- **Key people:** John McCarthy, Donald Knuth (in early years),
  Les Earnest, various AI researchers

### 7. INRIA (Institut National de Recherche en Informatique et en Automatique)
   (`INRIA.json`)

- **Founded:** 1967 (France)
- **Key contributions:**
  - Prolog (Alain Colmerauer, University of Marseille/INRIA affiliation)
  - OCaml (Xavier Leroy, Caml Light lineage from LCF-ML)
  - Coq proof assistant (Thierry Coquand, Christine Paulin, INRIA)
  - Caml and its ML lineage
  - Formal verification research that produced CompCert
- **Key people:** Alain Colmerauer, Xavier Leroy, Gérard Huet
- **Legacy:** France's INRIA is responsible for OCaml and Coq — two tools
  that underpin modern formal verification, Jane Street's trading
  infrastructure, and the Rust compiler's formal safety proofs.

## Required JSON Schema

Per AGENT.md, org profiles require:

```json
{
  "title": "Organization Name: Short Descriptor",
  "founded": "Year or 'Year (as ...)'",
  "overview": "2-3 sentence summary of the org's mission and primary
    computing contributions.",
  "key_contributions": [
    "**Contribution name (year):** One-sentence impact.",
    "..."
  ],
  "pivotal_people": [
    "**Name:** Role and contribution.",
    "..."
  ],
  "legacy": "150+ words on lasting impact on programming languages, paradigms,
    or computing infrastructure. Be specific about what living systems
    trace their lineage here.",
  "ai_assisted_discovery_missions": [
    "1. 'Quoted research prompt.'",
    "2. '...'",
    "3. '...'"
  ]
}
```

Do not add fields beyond those listed. The `key_contributions` and
`pivotal_people` fields accept arrays of formatted strings (same pattern
as `pivotal_works` in people profiles).
